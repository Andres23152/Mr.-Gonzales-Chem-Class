Net Ionic Equations ▼

Net ionic equations focus on the chemical species that are directly involved in a reaction, excluding the spectator ions. This simplifies the equation to its essential components.

Example: For the reaction between silver nitrate and sodium chloride:

AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO3(aq)

The net ionic equation is:

Ag+(aq) + Cl-(aq) → AgCl(s)

Ionic Bonds ▼

Ionic bonds are formed through the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. This type of bonding typically occurs between metals and non-metals.

Evidence of Ionic Bonds:

  • Formation of a precipitate.
  • High melting and boiling points.
  • Conductivity in molten or dissolved states.

Precipitation Reactions ▼

Precipitation reactions occur when two aqueous solutions combine to form an insoluble solid, known as a precipitate.

Example: Mixing aqueous solutions of barium chloride and sodium sulfate results in the formation of barium sulfate precipitate:

BaCl2(aq) + Na2SO4(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq)

Acid-Base Reactions ▼

Acid-base reactions involve the transfer of protons (H+) between reactants. According to the Brønsted-Lowry theory, an acid donates a proton, while a base accepts a proton.

Example: Neutralization of hydrochloric acid with sodium hydroxide:

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)

The net ionic equation is:

H+(aq) + OH-(aq) → H2O(l)

Oxidation-Reduction Reactions ▼

Oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions involve the transfer of electrons between species. Oxidation refers to the loss of electrons, while reduction refers to the gain of electrons.

Example: Reaction between zinc and copper(II) sulfate:

Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)

Half-Reactions:

  • Oxidation: Zn(s) → Zn2+(aq) + 2e-
  • Reduction: Cu2+(aq) + 2e- → Cu(s)

Physical vs Chemical Changes ▼

Physical Changes: Changes that do not alter the chemical identity of a substance. Examples include changes in state, shape, or size.

Chemical Changes: Changes that result in the formation of new substances with different chemical properties. Examples include reactions that produce gas, color change, precipitate formation, or temperature change.

Examples:

  • Physical: Melting ice, dissolving sugar in water.
  • Chemical: Burning wood, reacting vinegar with baking soda.
