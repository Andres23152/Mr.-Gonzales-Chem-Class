Review of Key Concepts ▼

Exothermic vs. Endothermic Reactions

Exothermic reactions release heat to the surroundings (temperature rises) whereas endothermic reactions absorb heat (temperature falls). In calorimetry experiments, a rise in temperature indicates an exothermic process.

Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the measurement of heat transfer in chemical reactions. A coffee-cup calorimeter is used for reactions at constant pressure, while a bomb calorimeter is used for constant-volume reactions such as combustion.

Phase Changes and Heating Curves

Heating curves graphically show how a substance’s temperature changes as it is heated. Plateaus on the curve indicate phase changes (melting, boiling) during which energy is absorbed without changing temperature (latent heat).

Hess’s Law and Reaction Enthalpy

Hess’s Law states that the overall enthalpy change for a reaction is the sum of the enthalpy changes for the individual steps. It allows you to calculate unknown enthalpy changes using known formation or combustion data.

Bond Enthalpies

Bond enthalpy is the energy required to break one mole of bonds in the gas phase. Calculations using bond energies involve subtracting the energy of bonds formed from the energy of bonds broken to estimate the overall reaction enthalpy.

Acid–Base Reaction ▼

Acid–base reactions typically involve the neutralization of an acid by a base, resulting in the formation of water and a salt. The reaction is exothermic, releasing heat that can raise the temperature of the solution.

Dissolution of a Strong Base ▼

When a strong base like KOH dissolves in water, the process is exothermic. The dissolution releases heat, increasing the water’s temperature.

Self‐Heating Reaction ▼

Self-heating food containers use exothermic reactions (e.g., CaO with H₂O) to generate heat. The reaction releases energy that is transferred to the food, warming it.

Metal Calorimetry ▼

In metal calorimetry, a hot metal is placed in cooler water. Heat transfers from the metal to the water until equilibrium is reached. This is an application of energy conservation.

Mixing Solutions at Different Temperatures ▼

When two solutions of the same solute at different temperatures are mixed, energy is transferred from the warmer to the cooler solution until the temperatures equalize.

Mixing Samples of the Same Liquid ▼

Mixing two samples of the same liquid at different temperatures causes thermal energy to transfer from the warmer sample to the cooler one until equilibrium is reached.

Bomb Calorimetry ▼

Bomb calorimetry is used to measure the heat of combustion at constant volume. The temperature rise of the surrounding water (with known mass and specific heat) is used to calculate the heat released.

Conservation of Energy ▼

In any chemical reaction, the energy lost by one part of the system is gained by another. In calorimetry, the heat lost by a hot substance is equal (with opposite sign) to the heat gained by the cooler substance.

Dissolution Temperature Change ▼

When a substance dissolves and the solution’s temperature changes, the difference between the final and initial temperature (ΔT) indicates the heat absorbed or released during dissolution.

Particle Model for Phase Change ▼

In phase changes, such as evaporation and condensation, particles gain or lose kinetic energy. The energy required for evaporation is released when the vapor condenses.

Condensation Enthalpy Comparison ▼

Different substances release different amounts of energy upon condensation. Comparing the enthalpy values for H₂O and H₂S helps illustrate how many moles of a substance must condense to equal the energy release of another.

Heating Curve & Phase Change ▼

Heating curves illustrate the temperature change of a substance as heat is added. Plateaus indicate phase changes where energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces rather than increase temperature.

Reaction Enthalpy & Stoichiometry ▼

Reaction enthalpy (ΔH) is determined by the stoichiometry of the reaction. It represents the overall heat change and can be calculated using bond energies or standard formation values.

Thermite Reaction ▼

The thermite reaction (e.g., 2 Al + Fe₂O₃ → 2 Fe + Al₂O₃) is highly exothermic. It is a classic example of a reaction that releases large amounts of energy due to the formation of very stable products.

Enthalpy Change (Endothermic Reaction) ▼

Endothermic reactions absorb energy, resulting in a positive ΔH. When using calorimetry data, a drop in temperature of the surroundings can indicate that the reaction absorbed heat.

Bond Enthalpy in Ozone Conversion ▼

Bond enthalpy calculations can be used to estimate the energy of bonds within a molecule. In the conversion of ozone to oxygen, overall ΔH and the number of bonds are used to determine the average bond enthalpy.

Bond Enthalpy via Combustion Data ▼

Combustion reactions provide a means to calculate average bond enthalpies by comparing the energy required to break bonds in reactants with the energy released when new bonds are formed in products.

Reaction Enthalpy from Bond Energies ▼

The reaction enthalpy from bond energies is calculated by subtracting the total energy of bonds formed from the total energy required to break bonds. This method uses average bond enthalpy values to approximate ΔH.

Reaction Enthalpy from Formation Data ▼

Standard enthalpies of formation are used to calculate the reaction enthalpy. By summing the formation values of products and subtracting those of reactants, you can determine the overall ΔH of a reaction.

Combustion of Propane ▼

The combustion of propane is a common reaction in which propane reacts with oxygen to form CO₂ and H₂O. The reaction enthalpy can be calculated using the formation enthalpies of the reactants and products.

Hess’s Law – Additional Data ▼

When using Hess’s Law to calculate unknown enthalpy changes, additional data (such as a formation enthalpy) may be required to complete the reaction cycle.

Hess’s Law – Combining Reactions ▼

Hess’s Law allows you to add or subtract reactions to obtain the overall enthalpy change. The key is to ensure that the reaction steps, when combined, match the target reaction.

Hess’s Law – Required Formation Data ▼

To apply Hess’s Law fully, sometimes the standard enthalpy of formation of one or more compounds must be known. This information is necessary to calculate the overall reaction enthalpy.

  1. Acid–Base Reaction in a Coffee‐Cup Calorimeter
    When 50.0 mL of 1.0 M HNO₃(aq) at 22.0 °C is mixed with 50.0 mL of 1.0 M NaOH(aq) at 22.0 °C in a coffee‐cup calorimeter, the final temperature is 27.0 °C. Which conclusion is best supported by this observation?
    • A) The reaction is endothermic, because heat is absorbed from the surroundings.
    • B) The reaction is endothermic, because the reactants cool upon mixing.
    • C) The reaction is exothermic, because heat is released by the reaction and is absorbed by the solution.
    • D) The reaction is exothermic, because energy is absorbed by the reactants during mixing.
    Show Answer
    Answer: C
    Step-by-step Explanation: The neutralization reaction releases heat. The increase in temperature of the solution confirms that energy is released (exothermic process).
  2. Dissolution of a Strong Base
    Crystalline KOH(s) is added to water and the solution’s temperature increases noticeably. Which statement correctly explains the observation?
    • A) The process is endothermic because the water absorbs energy.
    • B) The process is endothermic because the KOH crystals absorb energy.
    • C) The process is exothermic because the water releases energy as the KOH dissolves.
    • D) The process is exothermic because the dissolution of KOH releases heat into the water.
    Show Answer
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Dissolving KOH is exothermic; the release of heat raises the water’s temperature.
  3. Self‐Heating Food Container Reaction
    A self-heating can of soup uses the reaction: CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(s) (ΔH° = –64 kJ/mol). Which statement best explains how this reaction heats the soup?
    • A) The reaction is endothermic, so it draws heat from the soup.
    • B) The reaction is exothermic, so it releases heat that warms the soup.
    • C) The reaction is endothermic, but the container’s insulation causes the soup to warm.
    • D) The reaction is exothermic, but it absorbs heat from the surroundings before warming the soup.
    Show Answer
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The reaction releases heat as CaO reacts with water; this heat directly warms the soup.
  4. Metal Calorimetry
    A 10.0 g copper pellet initially at 80.0 °C is dropped into an insulated cup containing 50.0 g of water at 20.0 °C. Which explanation best justifies the expectation that the water’s temperature will rise?
    • A) Thermal energy is transferred from the hot copper to the water through molecular collisions, increasing the water’s kinetic energy.
    • B) The copper’s electrons transfer energy to the water, breaking hydrogen bonds.
    • C) The water’s molecules lose potential energy as they interact with the copper’s lattice.
    • D) The copper pellet absorbs heat from the water, resulting in an increase in water temperature.
    Show Answer
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The hot copper transfers its thermal energy to the cooler water through collisions, raising the water’s temperature.

    Two aqueous solutions of KCl (same concentration and volume) are initially at 15.0 °C and 30.0 °C. When combined, which of the following occurs?
    • A) The particles in the cooler solution lose kinetic energy upon mixing.
    • B) The particles in the cooler solution gain kinetic energy until the system reaches thermal equilibrium.
    • C) The overall average kinetic energy becomes lower than that of the cooler solution.
    • D) The overall average kinetic energy becomes higher than that of the warmer solution.
    Show Answer
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The cooler solution’s molecules absorb energy from the warmer solution until both reach the same temperature.
  6. Mixing Two Samples of the Same Liquid

    • A) Energy is not exchanged because both samples are the same substance.
    • B) Energy is transferred from Sample 2 to Sample 1 due to its larger volume.
    • C) Energy is transferred because the higher-density sample loses thermal energy.

  8. Conservation of Energy in Calorimetry

    • A) –q₍Sn₎ = ½ q₍H₂O₎

    • D) –q₍Sn₎ = q₍H₂O₎

    Answer: D

  9. Dissolution Temperature Change
    A student dissolves 12.00 g of NH₄Cl(s) in 150.0 g of water in a coffee‐cup calorimeter. The temperature drops from 26.0 °C to 20.5 °C. What is ΔT (to the proper significant figures)?

  15. Enthalpy Change for an Endothermic Reaction

    • D) 100.0 kJ

    Explanation: Since the full reaction (using 3 moles of NO₂) absorbs 200 kJ, 0.50 mole of NO₂ will absorb approximately (0.50/3)*200 ≈ 33.3 kJ.

  17. Estimating Bond Enthalpy Using Combustion Data

    • A) ½[ΔH₍rxn₎ + 2(799) + 4(463) – 4(412)] kJ/mol

    Answer: A
