Practice Test ▼

Answer the following questions to test your understanding:

  1. Consider the gas‐phase reaction:
        2 A(g) + B(g) → AB₂(g)

    The following experimental data were obtained:

    Experiment    [A] (M)    [B] (M)    Initial Rate (M/s)
    1          0.10        0.10        1.0×10-3
    2          0.20        0.10        4.0×10-3
    3          0.20        0.20        8.0×10-3

    Which rate law is consistent with these data?

    • (A) Rate = k[A][B]
    • (B) Rate = k[A]²[B]
    • (C) Rate = k[A][B]²
    • (D) Rate = k[A]²[B]²
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[A]²[B]
    Explanation: Comparing Experiments 1 and 2, doubling [A] (with [B] constant) quadruples the rate, indicating second order in A. Comparing Experiments 2 and 3, doubling [B] (with [A] constant) doubles the rate, indicating first order in B.

  2. For the reaction:
        X(g) + Y₂(g) → XY₂(g)
    a chemist determines that the rate law is: Rate = k[X][Y₂].

    In a separate experiment, a catalyst Z is added while the concentrations of X and Y₂ are held constant, and the rate increases significantly. Which explanation best accounts for the increased rate?

    • (A) The catalyst increases the frequency of collisions by increasing the reactant concentrations.
    • (B) The catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
    • (C) The catalyst increases the concentration of Y₂ in the reaction mixture.
    • (D) The catalyst raises the temperature of the system.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) The catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
    Explanation: Catalysts speed up reactions by lowering the activation energy through an alternative pathway without changing the reactant concentrations or the overall rate law.

  3. For the reaction:
        2 D(g) → D₂(g)

    If the measured initial rate of formation of D₂ is 0.050 M/s, what is the corresponding rate of disappearance of D?

    • (A) 0.025 M/s
    • (B) 0.050 M/s
    • (C) 0.075 M/s
    • (D) 0.100 M/s
    Show Answer
    Answer: (D) 0.100 M/s
    Explanation: According to the stoichiometry, 2 moles of D are consumed for every 1 mole of D₂ produced. Therefore, the rate of disappearance of D is 2 × 0.050 M/s = 0.100 M/s.

  4. A reaction involving reactant E(g) is studied. The following plots are obtained:

    • [E] vs. time: curved
    • ln[E] vs. time: linear
    • 1/[E] vs. time: curved

    What is the order of the reaction with respect to E?

    • (A) Zeroth order
    • (B) First order
    • (C) Second order
    • (D) Third order
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) First order
    Explanation: A linear plot of ln[E] versus time indicates first-order kinetics with respect to E.

  5. The formation of HI from H₂ and I₂ is proposed to follow the mechanism:

    Step 1 (fast equilibrium): I₂ ⇌ 2 I
    Step 2 (slow): H₂ + I → HI + H
            H + I → HI

    By combining steps, the overall reaction is: H₂ + I₂ → 2 HI.
    Using the first two steps (with the slow step determining the rate), which of the following is the rate law (expressed in terms of [H₂] and [I₂])?

    • (A) Rate = k[H₂][I₂]
    • (B) Rate = k[H₂][I₂]1/2
    • (C) Rate = k[H₂][I₂]²
    • (D) Rate = k[H₂]1/2[I₂]
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[H₂][I₂]1/2
    Explanation: The slow step gives Rate = k₂[H₂][I]. From the fast equilibrium, K = [I]²/[I₂] so that [I] = (K[I₂])1/2. Substituting yields Rate = k' [H₂][I₂]1/2.

  6. Consider the mechanism for the reaction:
        J + 2 K → JK₂
    proposed to occur by:

    Step 1 (slow): J + K → JK
    Step 2 (fast): JK + K → JK₂

    What is the overall rate law for the reaction?

    • (A) Rate = k[J][K]²
    • (B) Rate = k[J][K]
    • (C) Rate = k[J]²[K]
    • (D) Rate = k[J]²[K]²
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[J][K]
    Explanation: Since the slow (rate-determining) step is J + K → JK, the rate law is given by Rate = k₁[J][K]. The fast step does not affect the overall rate law.

  7. A reaction involving L(g) is found to have a linear plot of 1/[L] versus time. What is the order of the reaction with respect to L, and how does the half-life (t1/2) depend on the initial concentration [L]0?

    • (A) First order; t1/2 is independent of [L]0.
    • (B) Second order; t1/2 ∝ 1/[L]0.
    • (C) Zeroth order; t1/2 ∝ [L]0.
    • (D) Second order; t1/2 is independent of [L]0.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Second order; t1/2 ∝ 1/[L]0.
    Explanation: A linear plot of 1/[L] versus time is characteristic of a second-order reaction, for which the half-life is given by t1/2 = 1/(k[L]0).

  8. The reaction 2 NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2 NO₂(g) is proposed to occur via the following mechanism:

    Step 1 (slow): NO + O₂ → NO₃ (intermediate)
    Step 2 (fast): NO₃ + NO → 2 NO₂

    Which rate law is consistent with this mechanism?

    • (A) Rate = k[NO]²[O₂]
    • (B) Rate = k[NO][O₂]
    • (C) Rate = k[NO₃][NO]
    • (D) Rate = k[NO][NO₂]
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[NO][O₂]
    Explanation: Because the slow, rate-determining step is NO + O₂ → NO₃, the rate law is directly determined by its stoichiometry: Rate = k[NO][O₂]. The intermediate NO₃ is not included in the overall rate law.

  9. For the reaction:
        P(g) + Q(g) → R(g)

    Experiments show that doubling [P] doubles the rate while doubling [Q] increases the rate by a factor of four. What is the experimental rate law?

    • (A) Rate = k[P][Q]
    • (B) Rate = k[P][Q]²
    • (C) Rate = k[P]²[Q]
    • (D) Rate = k[P]²[Q]²
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[P][Q]²
    Explanation: Doubling [P] (first-order) doubles the rate and doubling [Q] (second-order) quadruples the rate, resulting in Rate = k[P][Q]².

  10. In the heterogeneous reaction:
        S(s) + T(aq) → ST(aq)

    Two trials are performed. In Trial 1, a large chunk of S is used; in Trial 2, S is used in powdered form. Which trial will have the faster reaction rate, and why?

    • (A) Trial 1, because the larger piece of S has greater mass.
    • (B) Trial 2, because powdered S has a greater surface area for reaction.
    • (C) Trial 1, because the concentration of S is higher.
    • (D) Trial 2, because the powdered form increases the concentration of T.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Trial 2, because powdered S has a greater surface area for reaction.
    Explanation: Powdering a solid increases its surface area, allowing more reactant–solution contact and leading to an increased reaction rate.

  11. A reaction is found to be zero order in reactant U. Which statement best describes the effect of [U] on the rate?

    • (A) Doubling [U] doubles the rate.
    • (B) Doubling [U] quadruples the rate.
    • (C) The rate is independent of [U].
    • (D) The rate decreases as [U] increases.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (C) The rate is independent of [U].
    Explanation: In a zero-order reaction the rate does not depend on the concentration of the reactant.

  12. For the reaction:
        2 X(g) → X₂(g)

    If the rate of formation of X₂ is 0.080 M/s, what is the rate of disappearance of X?

    • (A) 0.040 M/s
    • (B) 0.080 M/s
    • (C) 0.120 M/s
    • (D) 0.160 M/s
    Show Answer
    Answer: (D) 0.160 M/s
    Explanation: Since 2 moles of X produce 1 mole of X₂, the rate of X disappearance is 2 × 0.080 M/s = 0.160 M/s.

  13. Which of the following best describes the effect of a catalyst on the reaction energy profile?

    • (A) It increases the activation energy, slowing the reaction.
    • (B) It provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
    • (C) It changes the overall free energy change (ΔG) of the reaction.
    • (D) It increases the energy of the reactants.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) It provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
    Explanation: Catalysts lower the activation energy by offering an alternative reaction pathway while leaving the overall thermodynamics (ΔG) unchanged.

  14. The overall reaction for the decomposition of compound Y is:
        2 Y → Products

    Experimental studies show that the reaction is first order in Y. Which mechanism is most consistent with this observation?

    • (A) A bimolecular collision between two Y molecules in the rate‐determining step.
    • (B) A unimolecular decomposition of Y in the slow step.
    • (C) A catalyst–mediated reaction where Y reacts with the catalyst.
    • (D) A simultaneous collision of two Y molecules in a fast step.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) A unimolecular decomposition of Y in the slow step.
    Explanation: Even though the overall stoichiometry involves two Y molecules, first-order kinetics implies that the slow, rate-determining step involves only one Y molecule (a unimolecular process).

  15. The synthesis of phosgene (COCl₂) is proposed to occur via the mechanism below:

    Step 1 (fast equilibrium): Cl₂ ⇌ 2 Cl
    Step 2 (slow): CO + Cl → COCl₂

    Expressed in terms of [CO] and [Cl₂], which rate law is consistent with this mechanism?

    • (A) Rate = k[CO][Cl₂]
    • (B) Rate = k[CO][Cl₂]1/2
    • (C) Rate = k[CO]²[Cl₂]
    • (D) Rate = k[CO][Cl₂]²
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[CO][Cl₂]1/2
    Explanation: From the fast equilibrium, [Cl] ∝ [Cl₂]1/2. The slow step gives Rate = k₂[CO][Cl]; substituting for [Cl] yields Rate = k' [CO][Cl₂]1/2.

  16. For a second-order reaction involving reactant D, the half-life (t1/2) is given by which expression?

    • (A) t1/2 = 0.693/k
    • (B) t1/2 = 1/(k[D]0)
    • (C) t1/2 = k[D]0
    • (D) t1/2 = 1/(2k[D]0)
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) t1/2 = 1/(k[D]0)
    Explanation: For a second-order reaction, the half-life depends on the initial concentration and is given by t1/2 = 1/(k[D]0).

  17. A first-order reaction has a measured half-life of 6.93 seconds. What is the rate constant k?

    • (A) 0.10 s-1
    • (B) 0.05 s-1
    • (C) 0.15 s-1
    • (D) 0.01 s-1
    Show Answer
    Answer: (A) 0.10 s-1
    Explanation: For a first-order reaction, t1/2 = ln 2/k. Thus, k = ln 2/6.93 s ≈ 0.693/6.93 s ≈ 0.10 s-1.

  18. Consider the mechanism for the reaction:
        A + B → C
    proposed to occur by:

    Step 1 (slow): A → I (intermediate)
    Step 2 (fast): I + B → C

    Why does the intermediate I not appear in the overall rate law?

    • (A) I is present in very high concentration.
    • (B) I is consumed only in the slow step.
    • (C) I’s concentration is determined by a fast equilibrium and can be expressed in terms of A.
    • (D) I is a catalyst in the reaction.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (C) I’s concentration is determined by a fast equilibrium and can be expressed in terms of A.
    Explanation: Intermediates are not included in the final rate law because they are eliminated by expressing their concentration in terms of the stable reactants using equilibrium relationships from fast steps.

  19. For the reaction:
        L(g) + M(g) → LM(g)
    which is catalyzed by substance X (which is not consumed), which statement is true regarding the catalyst X?

    • (A) X increases the equilibrium constant of the reaction.
    • (B) X is consumed during the reaction and then regenerated.
    • (C) X increases the activation energy of the reaction.
    • (D) X appears as a reactant in the overall rate law.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) X is consumed during the reaction and then regenerated.
    Explanation: A catalyst participates in the reaction mechanism but is regenerated by the end; it lowers the activation energy without affecting the overall equilibrium constant or appearing in the net rate law.

  20. An increase in temperature from 300 K to 310 K is observed to double the rate of a particular reaction. Which explanation best accounts for this observation?

    • (A) The increased temperature raises the concentration of the reactants.
    • (B) The increased temperature results in a larger fraction of molecules exceeding the activation energy.
    • (C) The increased temperature decreases the frequency of collisions.
    • (D) The increased temperature increases the equilibrium constant significantly.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) The increased temperature results in a larger fraction of molecules exceeding the activation energy.
    Explanation: Higher temperatures increase the number of molecules with energy equal to or greater than the activation energy, which increases the rate constant according to the Arrhenius equation.

  21. In a heterogeneous catalytic reaction on a solid surface, the catalyst becomes saturated with reactant. Under these conditions, what is most likely true about the reaction rate?

    • (A) It is directly proportional to the reactant concentration.
    • (B) It becomes independent of the reactant concentration (zero order).
    • (C) It increases exponentially with the reactant concentration.
    • (D) It is inversely proportional to the reactant concentration.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) It becomes independent of the reactant concentration (zero order).
    Explanation: When the surface is saturated (all active sites are occupied), further increases in reactant concentration do not affect the rate, resulting in zero-order kinetics.

  22. The dissociation of diatomic molecule A₂ into atoms A is proposed to occur by a unimolecular process:
        A₂ → 2 A

    Which rate law is consistent with a unimolecular (first-order) mechanism?

    • (A) Rate = k[A₂]
    • (B) Rate = k[A₂]²
    • (C) Rate = k[A]
    • (D) Rate = k√[A₂]
    Show Answer
    Answer: (A) Rate = k[A₂]
    Explanation: A unimolecular dissociation is first order with respect to the reactant, so the rate law is Rate = k[A₂], regardless of the fact that two atoms are produced.

  23. For the reaction:
        2 X(g) + Y(g) → XY₂(g)

    A mechanism is proposed as follows:

    Step 1 (fast equilibrium): X + Y → XY (intermediate)
    Step 2 (slow): XY + X → XY₂

    What is the overall rate law?

    • (A) Rate = k[X][Y]
    • (B) Rate = k[X]²[Y]
    • (C) Rate = k[X]²[Y]²
    • (D) Rate = k[X][Y]²
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[X]²[Y]
    Explanation: The slow step has rate = k₂[XY][X]. From the fast equilibrium, [XY] ∝ [X][Y]. Substituting gives Rate = k[X]²[Y].

  24. In the reaction:
        A + B → C

    It is observed that doubling [A] while halving [B] produces no net change in the rate. If the rate law is written as Rate = k[A]m[B]n, what must be the relationship between m and n?

    • (A) m = n
    • (B) m = 2n
    • (C) n = 2m
    • (D) m = 0
    Show Answer
    Answer: (A) m = n
    Explanation: Doubling [A] increases the rate by 2m; halving [B] decreases the rate by 2n. For the overall rate to remain unchanged, 2m × (1/2)n = 1, so m = n.

  25. Studies of the reaction 2 M → N reveal that doubling the concentration of M increases the rate by a factor of 4. Which rate law is consistent with this observation?

    • (A) Rate = k[M]
    • (B) Rate = k[M]²
    • (C) Rate = k[M]1/2
    • (D) Rate = k[M]³
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) Rate = k[M]²
    Explanation: A doubling of [M] leading to a fourfold (2²) increase in rate is characteristic of a second-order reaction in M.

  26. In the decomposition of ozone (O₃) on a catalyst, it is observed that the activation energy is significantly lower than in the uncatalyzed reaction, while the overall thermodynamic parameters (ΔH, ΔG) remain unchanged. Which statement best describes the role of the catalyst?

    • (A) It increases the concentration of O₃.
    • (B) It provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
    • (C) It is consumed during the reaction.
    • (D) It increases the equilibrium constant of the reaction.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (B) It provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
    Explanation: A catalyst lowers the activation energy by providing an alternative mechanism, thereby increasing the reaction rate without affecting the overall thermodynamics.

  27. Consider the unimolecular reaction:
        D → F
    which is proposed to occur by the mechanism:

    Step 1 (fast equilibrium): D ⇌ E (intermediate)
    Step 2 (slow): E → F

    What is the overall rate law, and why does it reflect the observed kinetics?

    • (A) Rate = k[D]², because two D molecules are involved.
    • (B) Rate = k[E], but E is not observed.
    • (C) Rate = k[D], because the slow step (E → F) is rate determining and [E] is proportional to [D].
    • (D) Rate = k, because the reaction is zero order.
    Show Answer
    Answer: (C) Rate = k[D], because the slow step (E → F) is rate determining and [E] is proportional to [D].
    Explanation: Even though an intermediate E is formed, the slow (rate-determining) step converts E to F. Since E is in equilibrium with D, its concentration is proportional to [D], yielding a first-order overall rate law: Rate = k[D].
